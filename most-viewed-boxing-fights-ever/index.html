The "Fight of the Century"

The "Fight of the Century" between Muhammad Ali and Joe Frazier took place on March 8, 1971, and was one of the most highly anticipated boxing matches of all time. Ali was returning to the ring after being stripped of his title for refusing to be drafted into the Vietnam War, and Frazier was the undisputed heavyweight champion. The fight was a back-and-forth battle, with both boxers delivering punishing blows throughout the 15 rounds. In the end, Frazier won by unanimous decision, cementing his place as one of the greatest boxers of all time.

The "Thrilla in Manila"

The "Thrilla in Manila" between Muhammad Ali and Joe Frazier took place on October 1, 1975, and is widely considered one of the greatest boxing matches in history. The two boxers had already fought twice before, with Frazier winning the first match and Ali winning the second. The "Thrilla in Manila" was the rubber match, and both men were determined to come out on top. The fight was grueling, with both boxers taking and delivering massive amounts of punishment. In the end, Ali won by TKO in the 14th round, but both men left the ring with newfound respect for each other.

The "Rumble in the Jungle"

The "Rumble in the Jungle" between Muhammad Ali and George Foreman took place on October 30, 1974, in Kinshasa, Zaire. The fight was historic for a number of reasons, including the fact that it was held in Africa and was one of the first major international sporting events to be televised live around the world. Ali was considered the underdog going into the fight, but he used his famous "rope-a-dope" strategy to tire Foreman out and then knocked him out in the eighth round.

The "Brawl in Montreal"

The "Brawl in Montreal" between Sugar Ray Leonard and Roberto Duran took place on June 20, 1980, and was a rematch of their first fight, which Duran had won by unanimous decision. The second fight was a much different affair, with Leonard using his speed and footwork to frustrate Duran and avoid his punches. In the eighth round, Duran famously turned to the referee and said "no mas," signaling that he had had enough and was quitting the fight. The "Brawl in Montreal" remains one of the most controversial fights in boxing history.
